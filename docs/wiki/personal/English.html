Apostrophes

Gates's vs. Gates' is a matter of taste.

Irregular verbs

"Good" vs. "well"

"Good" is an adjective. "Well" is a verb. Think: "you fight good" vs. "you fight well." Informal usages of good and well are as adverbs

"I'm good" works because it's an adjective. "I'm well" is fine if you're referring to health.

"Lie" vs. "lay"

As a verb, "lie" means "to be in or assume a horizontal position." "Lay" is a verb with a direct object.

Initials

"J.K. Rowling" keeps the first name on the same line, due to word-wrapping on the web.

Numbers

The cut-off for spelled numbers is a matter of taste (nine or lower, ten or lower, etc.). But stay consistent with all subsequent ones if they're the same units.

Parentheses

Complete sentences get periods inside. Parenthetical expressions ending a sentence get periods outside.

Pronunciation

The built-in Mac and Kindle dictionaries use Oxford's traditional respelling, which is likely COD (Concise Oxford English Dictionary).

IPA is composed of modified Latin/Greek alphabets. The Latin consonants correspond to French and most European languages. The Latin vowels correspond to Spanish.

Quotation marks

The placement of other punctuations

Commas and periods always go inside the quotation marks in American English; dashes, colons, and semicolons almost always go outside the quotation marks.

source

Even in a long list of quoted items.

Exclamation and question marks go based on whether the quoted passage is the question/exclamation.

Quoting thoughts:

If you’re writing fiction, you may style a character’s thoughts in italics or quotation marks. Using italics has the advantage of distinguishing thoughts from speech.

Her footsteps echoing across the poorly lit, deserted alley, Julie thought to herself, Mother will be furious if she finds out I walked home instead of calling for a ride. Suddenly, she heard a voice behind her. “Julie! Why didn’t you wait for me?” In the darkness, Kayla, her cross-country teammate, came into focus. She had completely forgotten that Kayla had asked to walk home together.

Otherwise, when quoting a source, use quotation marks, and provide an indicator like, "she thought to herself."

Quoting direct questions:

If a direct question contained in a sentence is long or has internal punctuation, set the question off with a comma and begin it with a capital letter:

The question posed to the MLA editors was, How should a question contained in a sentence be punctuated?

The teacher wondered, Will my students ever understand how to incorporate a question in a sentence, or will they always do it incorrectly?

A single question contained in a sentence can also be preceded by a colon as long as the word before the question is not a verb. The question should start with a capital letter:

The answer left us with another question: When can a question be preceded by a colon?

Use lowercase letters to begin questions incorporated in series in a sentence:

Should I punctuate a question contained in a sentence with a comma? with a colon? with a dash?

In the example above, the questions in series are not capitalized because they are not complete sentences and do not begin with a proper noun.

But questions in series that are complete sentences are usually more appropriately styled with an initial capital letter:

I have several questions: What punctuation should I use? In what circumstances should I use it? What are the rules?
